About PIXLISE

PIXLISE streamlines the search for life on Mars by helping scientists understand data from the PIXL instrument flying on NASA's Perseverance Rover.

PIXLISE gives astrobiologists flexible and powerful visual analytics tools to quickly analyze and understand high-density, spatially-localized, imaging and XRF spectroscopy data

A collaboration between scientists, engineers and designers, PIXLISE was born out of Data to Discovery incubation program for science analysis tools, grew as part of JPL's Data Science Program, and elaborated into a robust tool through a multi-year partnership between Queensland University of Technology (QUT) and the Jet Propulsion Laboratory, California Institute of Technology.

PIXLISE depends upon Piquant, an X-ray fluorescence quantification tool developed by Tim Elam and Chris Heirwegh that uses the fundamental parameters physics model to translate spectral peaks into elemental abundances. It will soon be released as open source and a publication is in progress.

PIXLISE is built with open source tools, and will be open sourced after its main development.

Visualisation Capabilities

Context Imagery

PIXLISE enables the visualisation of co-registered context images as well as beam locations and regions of interest

Chord Diagram

The chord diagram allows science team members to see the spatial co-location of elements and minerals, supporting sample exploration

Interactive Plotting

Classic geology plots like ternary and binary diagram are made interactive, and can visualized regions of the sample, as well as the whole dataset.

Region Specific Spectra

Bulksum spectrums from any region in the dataset can be visualized together in order to compare specific areas, or quantify elements
